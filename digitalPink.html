Pink Art

An exploration of the algorithms supporting the curation process.

Pink Art

Algorithms are sets of rules to be followed in problem-solving operations, especially by a computer.

Curation is the collecting, sorting, and care of tangible assets, traditionally in a library or museum.

Pink Art considers the museum’s collection through the lens of a single color to explore the overlaps and tensions between two creative practices from different disciplines: curating and writing algorithms. The exhibition’s works of art were determined by ve algorithms written by faculty and students to grade the “pinkness” of works from the museum’s collection.

These algorithms begin with assumptions to instruct the computer’s operations; for example, the ISLANDIZE algorithm divides each image into separate shapes and reports the percentage of shapes that are pink. Because each starting assumption is di erent, the algorithms often disagree on the relative pinkness of a work of art. Often, the algorithms selected works our own eyes do not consider pink at all.

Set against the computer-based selection of works of art in this room is a trio of paintings, two of which were judged “less pink” by the algorithms. These three works, chosen by the exhibition’s curators, assert the eshy, material, and characteristic qualities of the color pink in works of art.

Chosen by different methods and for distinct reasons, the selection brought together here ruminates on the highly subjective interpretations and associations of color by machines, artists, and viewers.

Christina Olsen, former Class of 1956 Director
